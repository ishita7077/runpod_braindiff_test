Research

Why self-report misses.

Roughly 90% of decisions happen below conscious awareness — driven by instinct, emotion, and memory. Asking someone why they chose, liked, or bought returns a story the conscious brain wrote after the fact. BrainDiff skips the story and looks at the response.

The gap

The brain acts first. The mouth explains after.

Traditional market research asks a conscious mind to report on a subconscious process. That's asking consumers to act as their own psychologists — and it rarely works.

When researchers compare what people say they'll do with what they actually do, self-reported data only predicts real-world behaviour around 24% of the time. When they compare neural measures (fMRI, EEG) to real-world behaviour, prediction jumps to about 86%.

That gap is the reason BrainDiff exists.

What the cortex actually tracks

Three signals drive behaviour.

Decades of neuroscience, and more recently neuromarketing, converge on three signals that predict what people do next:

Personal relevance — activity in medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) tracks whether a message feels like it's about you. Falk et al. (2012) showed mPFC activation predicted population-level smoking-cessation response even when self-reports didn't.

Memory encoding — activity in the ventrolateral prefrontal cortex during exposure predicts whether content will be remembered days later. Memory is the bridge between seeing a message today and acting on it tomorrow.

Emotional + visceral response — the anterior insula and related regions fire on gut reactions: urgency, stakes, moral weight. This is the "felt edge" of a message that self-report usually flattens out.

BrainDiff reports on all three — plus four more (social reasoning, language depth, attention, processing effort) — every time you run a comparison.

The model

TRIBE v2 — Meta FAIR's open brain-response model.

BrainDiff is not a scanner. We don't record your brain. Instead, we borrow a model trained on other people's brains — Meta FAIR's TRIBE v2, released in 2025.

TRIBE v2 was trained on fMRI recordings from 8 subjects watching hours of naturalistic video and listening to rich narrative audio. From that data, it learned a mapping: given any piece of content, predict the cortical response pattern across the standard fsaverage5 surface — 20,484 vertices covering the whole cortex.

We take that prediction, run it for two pieces of content side-by-side, and subtract. The result: a signed map of where, and how strongly, one version moves the cortex more than the other.
