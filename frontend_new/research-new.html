A Brain Diff Research Note

What if the part that knows what matters
isn't the part you can ask?

One question, followed end to end. Stay with it — or skip to the parts that matter to you. Most names below don't need memorizing.

A note on language: when we say brain, we mean cortex — the outer sheet where most of what we call thinking happens.

↓ Begin
SECTION 01 · THEREFORE
→ THEREFORE

We'd need to know what "the brain" actually is.

The cortex is the outermost sheet of the brain. About two to four millimeters thick. Folded so densely that, unfurled, it would cover roughly the area of a dinner napkin. It is where the things we mean when we say thinking happen.

For research purposes, the cortex is mapped onto a standardized surface called fsaverage5: a reference template with 20,484 vertices, each one a point on the cortical sheet that can be tracked across subjects. When neuroscientists talk about “a region of the brain doing X,” they are usually talking about the average activity at some collection of these vertices, across many people, doing X.

In 2011, Yeo and colleagues at Harvard published a parcellation of this cortical sheet into seven canonical functional networks[1] — visual, somatomotor, dorsal attention, ventral attention, limbic, frontoparietal, default. They found these networks not by deciding what the cortex should look like, but by letting the cortex's resting activity reveal its own organization. The seven-network map is now one of the most reproduced findings in cognitive neuroscience.

You don't need to memorize the names. The cortex has structure — and that structure was discovered, not invented. That's the only thing this section is here to say.

SECTION 02 · BUT
↺ BUT

We don't have one brain. We have billions, all different.

Emotional intelligence has been an academic field for thirty-five years. Three measurement traditions dominate it. None of them agree with each other.

Ability tests like the MSCEIT score people on emotion problems, where the “correct” answer is whatever the majority of experts say.[2] The scoring is consensus-based, which is to say circular. Self-report questionnaires like the EQ-i and TEIQue ask people what they think they're good at — measuring not capacity but reputation, and overlapping heavily with personality and IQ.[3] Informant ratings like the ESCI ask the people around someone what they think — measuring social legibility, which is real but not the same as emotional intelligence itself.

Each tradition produces a number. The numbers don't correlate well with each other. After thirty-five years and dozens of meta-analyses, the field cannot say what its central object is.

The deeper problem isn't methodological. It's conceptual. Consider a simple test item:

Marcus's father just died. What should you say to him?

In an American workplace, the right answer is something direct: I'm so sorry. Let me know if there's anything I can do. In a Japanese workplace, the right answer is silence and a formal condolence card; direct emotional language would be intrusive. A stoic colleague might say the right answer is to ask Marcus about a work project — distraction as kindness. A therapist might ask Marcus what was coming up for him.

All four are defensible. Emotion theory does not pick a winner. Any “correct” answer the test assigns is smuggling in a cultural norm, a professional norm, or an individual preference. This is the structural reason the field stays stuck: the interesting questions don't have correct answers.

In 2023, a benchmark called EQ-Bench began scoring large language models on emotional reasoning tasks. The benchmark correlated 0.97 with MMLU, the standard general-knowledge benchmark.[4] Whatever EQ-Bench was measuring, it was almost identical to general reasoning. There was no separable emotional construct. The field had been measuring general cognitive ability with emotional wallpaper.

Three years earlier, a review in Biological Psychology had said it plainly:

No brain-based model of emotional intelligence
has yet been proposed.
Smith, Killgore & Lane · Biological Psychology · 2018

That sentence has stood for eight years. It still stands.

SECTION 03 · THEREFORE
→ THEREFORE

We'd need a brain that wasn't anyone's brain.

If individual brains vary, and self-report can't be trusted, the move is to step back from individuals altogether. To ask a different question.

For decades, fMRI researchers have been doing this without naming it. They aggregate across subjects to find what is universal — what the cortex does on average, regardless of who is in the scanner. Individual brains differ enormously in their fine-grained activity. The response patterns across cortex turn out to be surprisingly stable.

This is the move Brain Diff inherits. We are not measuring your brain. We are predicting the response of the average human cortex across a population of 720+ subjects.[5] When we say "the cortex did X," we mean: across the people whose brains were measured by Meta FAIR's training set, the cortex on average did X.

For content evaluation, this is exactly the right unit of analysis. The question someone making a video, an ad, a piece of writing actually wants answered isn't how will this one specific person respond? — it's how will humans, on average, respond? A focus group is one (broken) way to ask this. The cortex, simulated at the population level, is another.

This means we can't tell you about your individual variance. That is a real limitation, and we will return to it. For now: population-average is a feature.

SECTION 04 · BUT
↺ BUT

Aggregated brains don't watch real videos.
They lie in scanners doing lab tasks.

Putting a brain in a scanner costs roughly $600/hour. Recruiting subjects, running them, and analyzing the data turns a single behavioural study into a $150K–$300K, 6-to-12-month project. That cost is the reason almost everything we know about cognition was learned by handing people flashcards instead of movies.

The takeaway: Around 2004, Uri Hasson's lab at Princeton showed that when many people watched the same movie in a scanner, large swaths of cortex synchronized across them.[6] The cortex was responding to real content, not lab tasks — and that response was measurable, replicable, and population-wide.

For most of the previous history of cognitive neuroscience, the way you studied a brain was by giving it a tightly controlled task. Identify the face. Remember the word. Solve the puzzle. Beautiful science — reproducible, falsifiable. It also bore almost no resemblance to how brains actually spend their time.

Hasson's paradigm — “naturalistic stimuli” — is the methodological bridge between the lab task and the real world. Without it, the seven cortical systems Brain Diff exposes would only be defined by how they respond to flashcards. With it, we can see them respond to content as it is actually consumed. The entire training-data regime Brain Diff inherits is downstream of this shift.

SECTION 05 · THEREFORE
→ THEREFORE

We'd need to predict the cortex's response
without putting anyone in a scanner.

fMRI is expensive, slow, and fundamentally doesn't scale. A serious comparison tool — one that could be used outside research labs, on real content, by people who aren't neuroscientists — needs a model that predicts brain response from input. Not a measurement. A simulation.

Between 2021 and 2024, a small wave of papers established that this was possible.

In 2021, Schrimpf and colleagues at MIT showed that the internal representations of large language models — GPT-2, BERT, and others — predicted human brain activity in language regions, with surprising accuracy.[7] The same statistical structure that made language models work showed up in cortex.

In 2022, Goldstein and colleagues at Princeton went further: they argued that LLMs and human brains share computational principles for language — predicting the next word, integrating context, building meaning across time.[8] Not just similar outputs. Similar processing.

That same year, Caucheteux and King in Paris formalized a metric called “brain score”: a number indicating how brain-like a model's representations are.[9] A way to benchmark not what models can do, but how closely they resemble what cortex does when handed the same input.

In 2023, Tang and the Huth lab at UT Austin demonstrated semantic decoding from fMRI: continuous reconstruction of language from brain recordings, using a language model as the decoding bridge.[10] Not telepathy — but a clear demonstration that the mapping between cortex and learned model representations was real, bidirectional, and useful.

Each paper added one move. Each one made the next one possible. By 2024, the question wasn't whether encoding models could predict brain response. It was whether you could extend the technique beyond text — to images, to audio, to video.

SECTION 06 · THEREFORE
→ THEREFORE

TRIBEv2 — the model that ties it together.

In late 2025, Meta FAIR published TRIBEv2. It won the Algonauts 2025 brain-prediction challenge and was the integration the previous five years had been pointing at.

Video is the hard case. Audio, visuals, and language arrive simultaneously and interact — change the narration over the same image and the cortex responds differently. TRIBEv2 handles all three streams together. Three frozen feature extractors (LLaMA 3.2-3B, V-JEPA2, Wav2Vec-BERT) process each stream; a temporal transformer fuses them; a subject-specific projection maps the fused signal onto cortex.

Output, per input video: 20,484 cortical points × 1Hz of predicted BOLD response. Trained on 720+ subjects watching naturalistic content. Released CC BY-NC. Brain Diff is built on it.

Honest disclosure: this is predicted response, not measured. Out-of-distribution stimuli — pure music, abstract animation, content unlike the training set — produce noisier predictions.

What does the cortex say?

We have the cortex. We have a way to simulate its response. We can ask any video, any piece of audio, any combination of the two: what did the cortex do?

Take two pieces of content. Run each through TRIBE. Surface the seven cortical systems' response, second by second. The two responses can now be compared, system by system, moment by moment.

SECTION 09

So we built Brain Diff.

Upload two pieces of content. Each runs through TRIBEv2. The seven cortical systems respond, second by second. We line the responses up — and show you where they differ.

Brain Diff returns a description, not a verdict. A holds attention longer. B hits harder emotionally. Both engage language similarly. Where another tool would call a winner, Brain Diff shows you the shape of the difference and lets you make the call.

It doesn't rank, judge, or predict virality. It's the first encoding-model-based content tool we know of — built because the thing we wanted to use didn't exist, and the science said it should.

SECTION 10

The open research front
— and what's permanently outside the scope.

Two things to be honest about: what Brain Diff structurally cannot do, and the questions it newly makes askable. Both are open. Both are yours, if you want them.

Permanently outside the scope

No subcortical structures. TRIBE outputs cortical surface only. The nucleus accumbens, amygdala, and thalamus — reward, threat, arousal — are not visible to us. We use cortical proxies where we can (mPFC for value, anterior insula for visceral response). Knutson's NAcc-purchase work[11] is precedent, not inheritance.

No individual brains. Population-average across 720+ subjects. We can tell you what humans on average do — not what you do.

No sub-second resolution. 1Hz. Micro-expressions and 200ms audio cuts smear together.

Limited out-of-distribution generalization. Pure music, abstract animation, anything unlike the naturalistic-film training set — predictions get noisier. We label this when it applies.

Live calibration. We don't yet have ground-truth fMRI vs. Brain Diff predictions on identical content. We're building it. Until then, pattern thresholds are open-source defaults, not measured precision values. They will be revised as data accumulates.

Open research front

Questions Brain Diff makes newly tractable. Not a roadmap — a research agenda. If you're a researcher, builder, journalist, or critic, these are yours to take.

Q1
Does narrative structure produce predictable cortical signatures across cultures?
Q2
Can we identify when a piece of content is "memorable" before any audience has seen it?
Q3
What does it mean when two pieces of content produce near-identical cortical responses despite being completely different on the surface?
Q4
Is there a "minimum viable signature" — the shortest content that still produces a complete cortical response pattern?
Q5
Do creators with track records of resonant work share a cortical-response fingerprint?
Q6
How does the cortex respond to AI-generated content differently from human-generated content, if at all?
Q7
Can we identify cortical signatures of manipulation versus genuine resonance — or are they indistinguishable at this resolution?
