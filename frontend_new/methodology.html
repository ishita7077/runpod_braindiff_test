Methodology

How a BrainDiff is computed.

Every comparison runs through the same three-stage pipeline. The output is a signed cortical contrast — not a score, not a verdict. A map of where two pieces of content diverge in the brain.

The pipeline

Three stages, one contrast map.

Stage 1
Encode each text with TRIBE v2

Meta FAIR's open brain-response model converts each piece of content into a predicted response pattern across the cortex. TRIBE v2 was trained on fMRI from 8 subjects watching hours of naturalistic video and listening to narrative audio.

Tang et al., Meta FAIR · 2025
Stage 2
Project onto the fsaverage5 surface

Both predictions are mapped onto the standard 20,484-vertex cortical mesh, then contrasted vertex-by-vertex. Positive values mean text B evokes a stronger response at that vertex; negative values mean text A does.

HCP MMP1.0 atlas · Glasser et al., 2016
Stage 3
Decompose into seven systems

A Bayesian model average assigns each cortical region to one of seven functional systems mapped from the literature. The result: a seven-number readout that tells you why the brain is responding differently, not just where.

Yeo et al., 2011 · Power et al., 2011
The seven systems

What each number in your readout means.

Every BrainDiff result includes a seven-dimensional decomposition. Each number answers a different question about how the cortex is responding.

Attention

Dorsal attention network

What the reader selects and holds. Novelty, urgency, and contrast pull activity here (Corbetta & Shulman, 2002).

Memory encoding

Left ventrolateral prefrontal cortex · vlPFC

A cortical driver of what tends to stick. Two versions can feel equivalent in the moment and differ sharply in what the reader will remember tomorrow (Paller & Wagner, 2002).

Personal relevance

Medial prefrontal cortex · mPFC

Tracks whether a message feels like it's about you. Stronger activation here has predicted real-world behaviour change at population scale (Falk, 2012).

Gut reaction

Anterior insula

The visceral, felt edge of a message. Sensitive to urgency, stakes, moral weight, personal risk (Craig, 2009).

Social reasoning

Temporoparietal junction · TPJ

The cortical system for modelling other minds — perspective-taking and social inference (Saxe & Kanwisher, 2003).

Language depth

Broca's + Wernicke's network

Structural and semantic language processing. Deeper activation means more meaning is being extracted from the text itself (Fedorenko et al., 2010).

Processing effort

Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex · dlPFC

How hard the brain is working to parse the message. Higher isn't automatically worse — context-dependent (Owen et al., 2005).

Scope

What BrainDiff does not claim.

It's a lens, not a verdict. Four limits worth stating clearly up front.

Not a diagnosis.

BrainDiff is a simulation of a population-average cortical response. It does not evaluate, diagnose, or describe any individual reader.

Not a scan.

We don't record your brain. The model was trained on 8 subjects; the output is what an average cortex in that training distribution would predictably do.

Not a "good/bad" score.

Stronger response isn't automatically better. Strong gut reaction is useful for emergency messaging and wrong for a patient-consent form. Context matters.

Not about individuals.

Two readers will diverge from the population average in their own ways. BrainDiff tells you how two versions differ at the average — it cannot tell you how one specific reader will respond.
