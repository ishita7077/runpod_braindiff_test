Methodology · Patterns

How Brain Diff detects patterns.

Each named pattern in your result is a co-activation rule grounded in published cognitive neuroscience. This page is the inspectable record: the exact algorithm, the threshold values, and the papers behind every pattern. If anything here looks wrong, the source-of-truth file lives at data/pattern-definitions.json — open it, copy it, audit it.

Honest limits. These are population-average predictions from TRIBE v2 — a model trained on fMRI data from many subjects watching naturalistic media. They are a vocabulary for talking about what your content does to a typical cortex, not a diagnosis of any individual viewer. They do not capture sub-second dynamics. They are correlations and published findings, not lab-replicated measurements of your audience.

The algorithm

Pattern detection is deterministic. No machine learning. Same input, same output, every time.

for each pattern in pattern-definitions.json: for each second t in the run: if every contributing dimension at t is ≥ its threshold: mark t as "in pattern" group runs of consecutive in-pattern seconds into blocks drop blocks shorter than min_duration_seconds for each remaining block, find the second with the highest mean activation across contributing dimensions and call it the peak

The full implementation is at backend/pattern_detector.py and is ~150 lines of pure Python. It reads pattern-definitions.json directly, so the science cannot drift between backend and frontend.

How thresholds were chosen

The v1 thresholds (typically 0.50–0.55) sit at roughly the 60th–70th percentile of activation observed across our demo result corpus. They are deliberately conservative — high precision, lower recall — so we under-call patterns rather than over-claim them. We ship a recalibration script at scripts/calibrate_patterns.py that re-derives thresholds from any new result corpus, and the JSON's calibration block documents the procedure.
