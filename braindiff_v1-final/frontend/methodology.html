How Brain Diff Works

Brain Diff compares two pieces of text by predicting how an average human brain responds to each one. It reports where Version B is higher, where Version A is higher, and where differences are too small to trust.

1) What Brain Diff does

Brain Diff takes two texts and runs each through TRIBEv2 to estimate population-level neural response patterns. We then compute signed contrasts across five interpretable dimensions and show the difference.

2) Where brain data comes from

The model is based on fMRI data from 700+ participants in prior neuroimaging datasets and maps text through a text-to-speech pipeline before prediction. This aligns comparisons to the speech-driven setup used in model development.

3) What the 20,484 numbers are

For each text, the model predicts activity over 20,484 cortical surface vertices. Think of these as many tiny points spread across the brain surface.

4) Why these 5 dimensions

We collapse vertex predictions into five cortical dimensions selected for interpretability and content relevance: Personal Resonance, Social Thinking, Brain Effort, Language Depth, and Gut Reaction.

5) How scoring works

Each dimension uses a signed mean over its region mask and is normalized within each stimulus using median scaling. The shown value is Version B minus Version A, so positive means B is higher.

6) What this is not

This is not a readout of any individual person's brain. It is not an engagement, virality, or conversion predictor and should not be interpreted as one.

7) Key studies in plain language

Research has shown that stronger activation in valuation and self-relevance regions can predict some behavior changes at the population level. In short: specific neural patterns can carry signal about what messages people act on (for example, Falk et al., 2012).

8) Whose brain is "average"

"Average" here means an aggregate from available study samples, which are often WEIRD-leaning (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic). Generalization limits apply across cultures, languages, and contexts.
